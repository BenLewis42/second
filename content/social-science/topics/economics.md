## Historical Systems

### Feudalism

A combination of legal, economic, military, cultural, and political customs that flourished in medieval Europe between the 9th and 15th centuries. A way of structuring society around relationships derived from the holding of land in exchange for service or labour. The classic definition describes reciprocal legal and military obligations of the warrior nobility, revolving around lords, vassals, and fiefs.

Since Elizabeth A. R. Brown's "The Tyranny of a Construct" (1974) and Susan Reynolds's *Fiefs and Vassals* (1994), there has been ongoing debate among medieval historians as to whether feudalism is a useful construct for understanding medieval society.

### Manorialism

The method of land ownership in parts of Europe, notably France and later England, during the Middle Ages. Defining features included a large manor house where the lord and his dependants lived, and a population of labourers or serfs who worked the surrounding land. Originated in the Roman villa system of the Late Roman Empire.

Manorialism outlasted feudalism as an economic system. As Andrew Jones noted: "it could maintain a warrior, but it could equally well maintain a capitalist landlord."

## Business Terms

- **Top line**: a company's gross revenues or sales
- **Bottom line**: a company's net income or profit
- **Three-sector model**: extraction of raw materials (primary), manufacturing (secondary), and service industries (tertiary)
- **Purchasing power parity (PPP)**: a measure of the price of specific goods in different countries, used to compare the absolute purchasing power of currencies

## Economic Theories

### Iron Law of Wages

Asserts that real wages always tend, in the long run, toward the minimum wage necessary to sustain the life of the worker.

### Supply-Side Economics

A macroeconomic theory postulating that economic growth can be most effectively fostered by lowering taxes, decreasing regulation, and allowing free trade. Based partly on the Laffer curve — a theoretical relationship between rates of taxation and government revenue.

A 2012 poll of leading economists found none agreed that reducing the US federal income tax rate would result in higher annual tax revenue within five years.

### Finance Capitalism

The subordination of processes of production to the accumulation of money profits in a financial system. The process of developing this kind of economy is called **financialization**. Thomas Palley has argued that the 21st century predominance of finance capital has led to a preference for speculation — Casino Capitalism — over investment for entrepreneurial growth.

**Reflexivity**: the self-reinforcing effect of market sentiment, whereby rising prices attract buyers whose actions drive prices higher still until the process becomes unsustainable (positive feedback loop).

### Shock Therapy

Policies intended to be implemented simultaneously to liberalize an economy: ending price controls, stopping government subsidies, privatizing state-owned industries, and tighter fiscal policies. First instance was neoliberal reforms of Chile under Pinochet, carried out by the Chicago Boys.

In post-Soviet Russia, neoliberal reforms resulted in a surge in excess mortality and decreasing life expectancy, along with rising economic inequality, corruption, and poverty. As Isabella Weber noted: "As a result of shock therapy, Russia experienced a rise in mortality beyond that of any previous peacetime experiences of an industrialized country."

### Planned vs Command Economies

**Planned economy**: distribution of goods and services takes place according to economic plans. Can be centralized, decentralized, participatory or Soviet-type. **Command economy**: government controls and regulates production, distribution, and prices, with substantial public ownership.

**Decentralized planning** has been proposed as a basis for socialism by anarchists, council communists, libertarian Marxists, and others. The possibility of a digital planned economy was explored in Chile (1971–1973) with Project Cybersyn.

### Export-Oriented Industrialization (EOI)

A trade and economic policy aiming to speed up industrialization by exporting goods for which the nation has a comparative advantage. Particularly characteristic of the East Asian Tigers: Hong Kong, Singapore, South Korea, and Taiwan.

### Agorism

A free-market anarchist political philosophy founded by Samuel Edward Konkin III. Divides people into an "economic class" (those who make their living through the market) and a "political class" (those who make their living by coercing others). Supports nonviolent overthrow of the second class by the first through peaceful black market and grey market activity, known as counter-economics.

## FIRE Economy

Any economy based primarily on finance, insurance, and real estate sectors. New York City's largest industry.
