## Overview

The scientific study of language. Based on a theoretical as well as a descriptive study of language, and interlinked with the applied fields of language studies and language learning.

Before the 20th century, linguistics evolved in conjunction with literary study and did not employ scientific methods. Modern-day linguistics is considered a science because it entails a comprehensive, systematic, objective, and precise analysis of all aspects of language — cognitive, social, cultural, psychological, environmental, biological, literary, grammatical, paleographical, and structural.

## Traditional Areas

- **Syntax**: rules governing the structure of sentences
- **Semantics**: meaning
- **Morphology**: structure of words
- **Phonetics**: speech sounds and equivalent gestures in sign languages
- **Phonology**: the abstract sound system of a particular language
- **Pragmatics**: how social context contributes to meaning

Subdisciplines such as biolinguistics (biological variables and evolution of language) and psycholinguistics (psychological factors in human language) bridge many of these divisions.

## Theoretical vs Applied

- **Theoretical linguistics** is concerned with understanding the universal and fundamental nature of language and developing a general theoretical framework for describing it.
- **Applied linguistics** seeks to utilise scientific findings for practical purposes, such as developing methods of improving language education and literacy.

## Study Approaches

Linguistic features may be studied through a variety of perspectives:
- **Synchronically** (structure at a specific point in time) or **diachronically** (historical development over time)
- In monolinguals or in multilinguals
- Among children or amongst adults
- Through written texts or through oral elicitation
- Through mechanical data collection or through practical fieldwork

Linguistics emerged from the field of **philology**. It is also related to [[philosophy]] of language, stylistics, rhetoric, semiotics, lexicography, and translation.

## Glossary

**Homophone**: A word that is pronounced the same as another word but differs in meaning. Examples: *rose* (flower) and *rose* (past tense of "rise"); *rain*, *reign*, and *rein*. Homophones that are spelled the same are both homographs and homonyms. Those spelled differently are called heterographs.
