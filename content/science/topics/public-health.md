Drug overdoses have [killed more Americans in the past 24 years](https://www.npr.org/2021/12/30/1069062738/more-than-a-million-americans-have-died-from-overdoses-during-the-opioid-epidemi) than [all U.S. wars combined](https://www.va.gov/opa/publications/factsheets/fs_americas_wars.pdf).

Noncommunicable diseases, partly due to a lack of exercise, are currently the greatest public health problem in most countries around the world. Each year at least 1.9 million people die as a result of physical inactivity, which makes inactivity one of the leading preventable causes of death worldwide.

Depression is the leading cause of disability worldwide.

## Obesity and Nutrition

- Americans eat 153 grams of sugar a day.
- Increase in American diabetes of 700%.
- U.S. food consumption: 3,782 kcal/capita/day (2018, Our World in Data).

Increasing rates of obesity at a societal level are felt to be due to an easily accessible and palatable diet, an increased reliance on cars, and mechanized manufacturing.

Other possible contributors to the recent increase of obesity include: insufficient sleep, endocrine disruptors (environmental pollutants that interfere with lipid metabolism), decreased variability in ambient temperature, decreased rates of smoking (because smoking suppresses appetite), increased use of medications that can cause weight gain (e.g., atypical antipsychotics), proportional increases in ethnic and age groups that tend to be heavier, pregnancy at a later age (which may cause susceptibility to obesity in children), epigenetic risk factors passed on generationally, natural selection for higher BMI, and assortative mating leading to increased concentration of obesity risk factors (this would increase the number of obese people by increasing population variance in weight).

Most of this extra food energy came from an increase in carbohydrate consumption rather than fat consumption. The primary sources of these extra carbohydrates are sweetened beverages, which now account for almost 25 percent of daily food energy in young adults in America, and potato chips.

## Economic Costs of Obesity

In 2005, the medical costs attributable to obesity in the US were an estimated $190.2 billion or 20.6% of all medical expenditures.

Obesity can lead to social stigmatization and disadvantages in employment. When compared to their normal weight counterparts, obese workers on average have higher rates of absenteeism from work and take more disability leave, thus increasing costs for employers and decreasing productivity. A study examining Duke University employees found that people with a BMI over 40 kg/m² filed twice as many workers' compensation claims as those whose BMI was 18.5–24.9 kg/m². They also had more than 12 times as many lost work days.
