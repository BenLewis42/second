# Measurement

**Measurement** assigns **numbers to properties** of the world using an agreed **unit** and **procedure**. Science depends on measurement when claims need to be checked, combined, or compared across labs and eras.

## International System of Units (SI)

Modern science and engineering mostly rely on the **SI**, built from **base quantities** (including length, mass, time, electric current, thermodynamic temperature, amount of substance, and luminous intensity) and **derived units** formed from them (e.g. energy in joules). Official definitions are updated over time so units can be tied to **constants of nature** rather than only to particular artifacts—an ongoing shift meant to improve long-term stability.

When you convert or compare results, carry **units explicitly** in calculation; most unit errors are caught early that way.

## Precision and uncertainty

Every measurement has **finite resolution**. Reporting should distinguish **precision** (repeatability) from **accuracy** (closeness to a defined reference). Error bars, significant figures, and stated uncertainties are not pedantry—they encode what the data actually supports.

## Specialized units

Some fields keep **non-SI units** for historical or intuitive reasons (electron-volts in particle physics, angstroms in crystallography, liters in everyday volume). The rule of thumb: use **SI in derivation and publication**, convert for intuition where helpful.

## Angstrom

An **ångström** (symbol Å) is a unit of length equal to **10⁻¹⁰ m**. It remains common for **atomic distances**, **crystal lattices**, and **electromagnetic wavelengths** in chemistry and materials science, even when SI (nanometers) is formally preferred in some contexts.
