## Schools

### Positive Psychology

Studies the conditions that contribute to the optimal functioning of people, groups, and institutions. Began as a new domain of psychology in 1998 when Martin Seligman chose it as the theme for his APA presidency. It is a reaction against past practices which tended to focus on mental illness and emphasized maladaptive behavior and negative thinking. Builds on the humanistic movement of Abraham Maslow, Rollo May, and Carl Rogers.

Largely relies on concepts from Western philosophical tradition, such as Aristotle's concept of eudaimonia. Factors that may contribute to happiness and subjective well-being include social ties, physical exercise, meditation, spiritual practice, and (up to a point) income.

### Depth Psychology

The conception that the psyche process is partly conscious, partly unconscious, and partly semi-conscious. Seeks to explore underlying motives as an approach to various mental disorders. Three main perspectives:

- **Psychoanalytic**: Melanie Klein, Donald Winnicott; object relations theory; Neo-Freudianism
- **Adlerian**: Alfred Adler's individual psychology
- **Jungian**: Carl Jung's analytical psychology; James Hillman's archetypal psychology

## Glossary

**Perseveration**: The repetition of a particular response regardless of the absence or cessation of a stimulus. More broadly, mental or physical behaviors which are apparently both functionless and involve a narrow range of behaviors.

**Father complex**: A group of unconscious associations pertaining to the image or archetype of the father. May be either positive (admiring and seeking out older father figures) or negative (distrusting or fearful). Central to the Freud/Jung split — Jung accused Freud of "treating your pupils like patients... meanwhile you are sitting pretty on top, as father."

**Father hunger** (James M. Herzog): The unconscious longing experienced by many males and females for an involved father. Margo D. Maine applied the concept to eating disorders, noting how father hunger prompted by paternal absence may leave the daughter with unhealthy narcissism and a prevalent search for external sources of self-esteem.

**Masking**: The process in which an individual camouflages their natural personality or behavior to conform to social pressures, abuse, or harassment. Can be strongly influenced by environmental factors such as authoritarian parents, rejection, and emotional, physical, or sexual abuse.

**Just-world hypothesis**: The cognitive bias that assumes "people get what they deserve" — that actions will necessarily have morally fair and fitting consequences. Often associated with rationalizing suffering on the grounds that the sufferers "deserve" it.

**Transitional object** (D.W. Winnicott): Something, usually a physical object, which takes the place of the mother-child bond. Common examples: dolls, teddy bears, blankets. Important at the time of going to sleep and as a defense against anxiety. Winnicott related this to the broader concept of transitional phenomena, which he considered the basis of science, religion and all of culture.

**Cathexis**: In psychoanalysis, the process of allocation of mental or emotional energy to a person, object, or idea. Freud described frustration of libidinal desires as a blockage of cathected energies which would eventually build up and require release in alternative ways — regression, sublimation, reaction formation, or symptom construction.

**Dissociative fugue**: A rare psychiatric phenomenon characterized by reversible amnesia for one's identity in conjunction with unexpected wandering or travel. Most commonly associated with childhood victims of sexual abuse who learn to dissociate memory of the abuse.

## Complexes

An example: if a person had a leg amputated when a child, they may develop many thoughts, emotions, memories, feelings of inferiority, triumphs, and determinations centering on that one aspect of life. The reality of complexes is widely agreed upon in depth psychology.

**Cinderella complex** (Colette Dowling): An unconscious desire to be taken care of by someone else. Named after the fairy tale character — an ideal of femininity where a woman is expected to be beautiful and supportive but fundamentally incapable of either taking care of herself or changing her situation through her own efforts.

## Classical vs Operant Conditioning

### Classical Conditioning (Ivan Pavlov)

Learning through association. An originally neutral stimulus becomes associated with a meaningful stimulus, eliciting a conditioned response.

- **Unconditioned Stimulus (US)**: Naturally elicits a response (e.g., food causing salivation)
- **Unconditioned Response (UR)**: Natural reaction to the US
- **Neutral Stimulus (NS)**: Initially does not elicit the UR (e.g., a bell)
- **Conditioned Stimulus (CS)**: The NS after association with the US
- **Conditioned Response (CR)**: The learned response to the CS

### Operant Conditioning (B.F. Skinner)

Learning through consequences.

- **Positive Reinforcement**: Adding a pleasant stimulus
- **Negative Reinforcement**: Removing an unpleasant stimulus
- **Positive Punishment**: Adding an unpleasant stimulus
- **Negative Punishment**: Removing a pleasant stimulus
- **Extinction**: Behavior stops when reinforcement ceases

## Misc

**Gunnysacking**: When someone silently collects irritations and slights until "the last straw" causes an overblown reaction.

**Stonewalling**: A refusal to communicate or cooperate. Tactics include giving sparse, vague responses; refusing to answer questions; and responding to questions with additional questions.
