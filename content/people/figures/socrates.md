## Overview

Socrates wrote nothing; we know him mainly through the dialogues of his student [[Plato]] and through Xenophon. He taught in the streets and agora of Athens, using relentless questioning (the Socratic method) to expose confusion and move others toward clearer definitions and moral understanding. He was tried and put to death by drinking hemlock. His focus on how we ought to live, and on the link between [[virtue]] and knowledge, made him a founding figure in Western ethics and [[epistemology]].

## Key Ideas

- **Socratic method (elenchus)**: Posing questions to test claims and definitions rather than lecturing. The aim was to reveal inconsistency and encourage better reasoning.
- **Virtue and knowledge**: Socrates held that no one does wrong willingly—wrongdoing stems from ignorance. So [[virtue]] is a kind of knowledge, and philosophy is a way of life aimed at that knowledge.
- **Care of the soul**: The most important task is to live well and tend to one's soul, not to pursue wealth or reputation.
- **"I know that I know nothing"**: Often summarized this way: wisdom includes recognizing the limits of one's knowledge.

## Connections

- **[[Plato]]** – Preserved and extended Socrates' thought; many "Socratic" dialogues likely reflect both historical Socrates and Plato's own development.
- **[[Aristotle]]** – Trained in the [[Academy]] founded by Plato; his ethics and method build on the Socratic-Platonic tradition.
