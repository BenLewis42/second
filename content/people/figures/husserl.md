## Overview

Husserl distinguished phenomenology as a science of pure consciousness from empirical psychology. Phenomenology is concerned with the essence of consciousness and intentionality, rather than the actual facts or realities of the external world.

Husserl emphasized the importance of distinguishing essential being from actual existence, and the pure ego from the psychological ego. Essences are non-real, while facts are real. Phenomenological reduction leads to knowledge of the essentially non-real, rather than the essentially real.

## Intentionality

The core of Husserl's phenomenology is the concept of intentionality — the idea that consciousness is always directed towards an object. Every act of consciousness (the "noesis") has an intentional object (the "noema").

## Phenomenological Method

Husserl described phenomenology as an a priori, eidetic science that examines the essences of phenomena through "bracketing" or suspending judgment about the existence of the external world. This allows phenomenology to focus on the pure intuition and acts of consciousness.

Overall, Husserl's main goal was to establish phenomenology as a rigorous, scientific philosophy that could provide a foundation for all other sciences by examining the essential structures of consciousness and experience.
