## Foucault's Concept of Unreason

- Foucault saw "unreason" as a broader category than just mental illness or madness. It encompassed various forms of irrationality, deviance, and transgression that challenged the dominant social order and norms of rationality.
- In the classical age (17th–18th centuries), Foucault argued that madness was understood in relation to this broader realm of "unreason", rather than just as a medical condition. Madness was seen as a manifestation of the "monstrous forms of animality" within unreason.
- Foucault believed that during this period, madness was not simply pathologized, but was seen as revealing a certain "liberty" or freedom that threatened the social order. It was this transgressive, scandalous aspect of madness that was isolated and confined in the classical age.

## Madness and the "Unreasonable"

- Foucault saw a shift in the modern era, where madness became increasingly medicalized and reduced to a deterministic "mechanism" rather than a form of "unreason."
- He argued that in the modern view, madness no longer disclosed a "liberty raging in the monstrous forms of animality", but rather a return to the "bleak world of beasts and things."
- For Foucault, the classical understanding of madness as a manifestation of a broader "unreason" was lost, and madness became simply equated with the "unreasonable" — a pathological deviation from rationality.

In summary, Foucault saw "unreason" as a crucial concept for understanding the classical view of madness, which he saw as a transgressive challenge to dominant social rationality. The shift towards a more medicalized, deterministic view of madness in the modern era was, for Foucault, a significant loss of this richer, more subversive understanding of the phenomenon.
