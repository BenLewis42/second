### Concepts

### Theory of Forms
Reality exists in two realms:
- **Material World**: Imperfect, constantly changing, perceivable through senses
- **Realm of Forms/Ideas**: Perfect, eternal, unchanging, perceivable only through reason

The Forms are the ultimate reality; physical objects are mere imperfect copies.

### The Cave Allegory
A foundational metaphor from the *Republic*:
- Prisoners chained in a cave can only see shadows on a wall, which they mistake for reality
- Philosophy is the painful journey toward enlightenment and seeing true reality
- Most people remain enslaved by [[illusion]]

### The Good
- The highest Form, the source of all truth and knowledge
- Like the sun in the visible world, enabling sight
- Understanding the Good is the ultimate goal of philosophy

### Justice in the Soul
Virtue arises from proper harmony of the soul's three parts:
- **Reason** (wisdom)
- **Spirit** (courage)
- **Appetite** (moderation)

Justice is their proper balance.

### Mathematical Proportion
Plato believed in the primacy of mathematical relationships and proportion as expressions of ideal Form.

### Influence on Art

Plato was skeptical of art, viewing it as thrice removed from truth:
- Truth → Forms (perfect reality)
- Forms → Physical objects (imperfect copies)  
- Physical objects → Artistic representations (copies of copies)

Yet paradoxically, his ideals deeply influenced artistic thinking:
- [[Renaissance]] artists sought to capture the "ideal" or perfect Form behind appearances
- The pursuit of ideal [[proportions]] and [[beauty]] traces back to Platonic philosophy
- [[Neoclassical art]] explicitly draws on Platonic aesthetics
- [[mathematical-proportion|Mathematical proportion]] became central to artistic practice

### Philosophy & Aesthetics

Plato's theory of Forms fundamentally shaped how artists think about:
- The relationship between appearance and reality
- The pursuit of ideal beauty rather than mere representation
- The ethical dimension of artistic creation
- The role of reason in creating art

His skepticism of imitation paradoxically made him essential to understanding art's purpose.
