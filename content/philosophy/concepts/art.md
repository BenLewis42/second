## Overview
Philosophy's relationship to art has been productive and antagonistic in roughly equal measure. [[plato]]'s suspicion of mimetic art — that it is twice removed from truth, imitating appearances rather than Forms — established a fault line that runs through aesthetics into the present. The [[renaissance]] reversed this suspicion, recasting the visual arts as disciplines requiring rigorous intellectual training in [[anatomy]], [[mathematical-proportion]], and natural philosophy. Art became a site where [[beauty]], understood as the sensory trace of ideal form, could be made available to perception and contemplation.

## Key Ideas
The central philosophical question about art concerns its relationship to truth: does art reveal or obscure reality? [[plato]]'s answer was largely negative, but his account of [[beauty]] as participation in the Form of the Beautiful gave later Neoplatonists grounds to rehabilitate visual art as a path toward the transcendent. [[renaissance]] theorists drew on this to argue that the painter or sculptor who masters proportion and observation approaches divine knowledge. The tension between art as [[ethics|ethical]] educator and art as autonomous aesthetic experience defines much of early modern and modern debate. [[beauty]] functions as the hinge concept — the question of whether beauty is objective or subjective ramifies into questions about the status of aesthetic judgment as a form of knowledge.
