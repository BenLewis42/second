## Overview
During the [[renaissance]], anatomy crossed from medical science into the workshop, driven by the conviction that accurate representation of the human form required direct knowledge of its inner structure. [[leonardo-da-vinci]] exemplifies this fusion most forcefully, conducting dissections and producing detailed drawings that served both scientific and artistic ends. This matters philosophically because it instantiates an empirical, observational epistemology within artistic practice — the artist as investigator, not merely craftsman. Anatomical knowledge became inseparable from [[mathematical-proportion]] as artists sought a unified rational account of the body.

## Key Ideas
The key shift was from idealized, schematic figures derived from classical statuary to bodies grounded in observed internal structure. [[leonardo-da-vinci]] documented musculature, bone articulation, and organ placement with a precision that anticipated modern anatomical illustration. Anatomical accuracy reinforced the broader Renaissance commitment to [[observation]] as a path to truth, aligning the visual arts with natural philosophy. The study of anatomy also had theological stakes: depicting the body correctly was, for many artists, a form of reverence for divine creation. [[mathematical-proportion]] and anatomy worked together — Vitruvian proportion systems described ideal ratios, while anatomical study verified and complicated them.
