## Overview

The **Theory of Forms** (or Ideas) is a central doctrine in **Plato’s** philosophy. In the dialogues, **Plato** argued that the objects we perceive are mutable and incomplete, whereas what is fully real must be stable and knowable.

The philosopher **Plato** developed this picture partly in reaction to **Heraclitean** flux and **Eleatic** arguments about being. Later **Aristotle** criticized the theory as positing a duplicate world of entities separate from sensible particulars.

## Core ideas

According to the theory, **Forms** are the perfect exemplars of properties such as justice, beauty, and equality. Particular things are said to **participate** in or **imitate** the Form, which explains why judgments of “more or less just” make sense.

The Form of the Good plays a special role in the *Republic* as the highest object of knowledge, though scholars disagree about how literally the dialogues intend the mathematical and metaphysical details.

## Reception

**Plato’s** students and later **Neoplatonists** reinterpreted the Forms in systematic metaphysics. In the 20th century, philosophers debated whether the theory is best read as logic of predication, as serious ontology, or as pedagogical myth.
