## Overview
Epistemology asks the most fundamental reflexive question in philosophy: what is knowledge, and how is it possible? [[ancient-greece]] established the canonical formulation — knowledge as justified true belief — but also generated the deepest challenges to it through Socratic argument and Skeptical schools. The relationship between epistemology and [[metaphysics]] is intimate: how one thinks the world is structured shapes what one takes it to mean to know something about it. [[plato]]'s theory of Forms made epistemology inseparable from a doctrine of two realms, while empiricist traditions grounded knowledge in sensory observation.

## Key Ideas
[[plato]]'s distinction between knowledge and mere opinion (doxa) remains the starting point: knowledge requires access to unchanging Forms, while sensation delivers only contingent appearances. [[socrates]]' method of elenchus operationalized epistemology as practice — demonstrating that interlocutors lack the genuine understanding they claim. The question of whether knowledge has foundations or is instead a web of mutually supporting beliefs divides foundationalists from coherentists, a debate that replays Greek tensions between [[metaphysics|metaphysical]] certainty and skeptical pressure. Epistemological questions also bear directly on [[art]]: can aesthetic judgment be a form of knowledge, and what would it mean for [[beauty]] to be known rather than merely felt?
