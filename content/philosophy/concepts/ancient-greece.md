## Overview
The philosophical culture of ancient Greece produced the first sustained attempts in the Western tradition to give systematic accounts of reality, knowledge, and value. [[socrates]], [[plato]], and Aristotle together defined the terms in which [[metaphysics]], [[epistemology]], and [[ethics]] would be debated for two millennia. This matters not only historically but structurally: much of subsequent Western philosophy is intelligible as a response to Greek problems — the nature of the Forms, the grounds of knowledge, the definition of the good life. The [[renaissance]] explicitly positioned itself as a recovery of this inheritance, and debates about [[beauty]] and [[art]] in the early modern period were largely conducted through Platonic and Neoplatonic frameworks.

## Key Ideas
[[plato]]'s theory of Forms is the most consequential contribution: the claim that particulars participate in universal, unchanging essences, and that genuine knowledge ([[epistemology]]) is of these Forms rather than of sensory particulars. [[socrates]] contributed the method of elenchus — rigorous cross-examination that exposes contradictory beliefs — establishing philosophy as a practice of critique rather than mere doctrine. Greek thought also anchored [[beauty]] in proportion and order, a connection that proved durably influential in [[art]] theory. Aristotelian philosophy offered a counter-tradition grounded in observation and empirical classification, which fed into both scientific and artistic naturalism during the [[renaissance]].
