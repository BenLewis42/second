## Overview
The tradition running from Kant through Hegel, Schopenhauer, Marx, and [[friedrich-nietzsche]] defines a distinct and dominant strand of [[19th-century]] philosophy that remains the primary reference point for continental thought. What unifies this tradition is less any single doctrine than a shared ambition: to give a total account of mind, history, culture, or will that comprehends its own conditions of possibility. German idealism's systematizing tendency provoked its own counter-movements — Schopenhauer's pessimistic philosophy of will, Kierkegaard's irrationalism, and Nietzsche's genealogical critique — making [[german-philosophy]] simultaneously the source of [[existentialism]] and its target.

## Key Ideas
Kant's critical philosophy established the framework by distinguishing what can be known from what can only be thought, grounding [[metaphysics]] in the conditions of possible experience rather than in claims about things-in-themselves. Hegel radicalized this by historicizing reason: truth unfolds through dialectical contradiction over time, and [[philosophy]] is the self-consciousness of this process. [[friedrich-nietzsche]] broke most sharply with the tradition's rationalism, arguing that [[power]] and drive underlie all allegedly rational systems, including morality. The [[ethics|ethical]] and [[existentialism|existentialist]] implications of this move proved enormously generative for 20th-century thought, from phenomenology to Frankfurt School critical theory.
