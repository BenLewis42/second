## Overview
Metaphysics asks the questions that precede and underlie all other philosophical inquiry: what is there, and what is it like? [[plato]]'s theory of Forms is the founding document of one major tradition, arguing that the most real entities are non-sensory universals, not the particular things we encounter in experience. [[german-philosophy]] in the [[19th-century]] both extended and attacked this tradition: Hegel gave metaphysics a historical form, while [[friedrich-nietzsche]] diagnosed metaphysical thinking itself as a symptom of life-denial, a flight from [[power]] and becoming into the fiction of stable essence.

## Key Ideas
The **problem of universals** — whether properties like redness or justice exist independently of particular things — runs through metaphysics from [[ancient-greece]] to the present. [[plato]]'s realism about Forms gave a strong affirmative answer; nominalist traditions deny it. [[ethics]] and metaphysics are entangled: [[plato]] held that knowledge of the Good is a metaphysical achievement, while [[existentialism]] insists that if metaphysical foundations are illusory, moral values must be humanly created. [[friedrich-nietzsche]]'s **will to power** reframes metaphysics as phenomenology of force — being is not static substance but dynamic striving — a move that influenced Heidegger's fundamental ontology and subsequent continental [[philosophy]].
