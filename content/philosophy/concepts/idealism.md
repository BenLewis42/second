## Overview

**Idealism** is a family of views in which reality, or our knowledge of it, is fundamentally tied to **minds**, **ideas**, or **spirit** rather than to matter standing wholly apart from cognition. The label covers ancient, early modern, and German traditions that are not a single school.

**George Berkeley** argued that to be is to be perceived, or perceived by God, and thereby rejected material substance as an unknowable substratum. **Immanuel Kant** is often called a **transcendental idealist** because he treated space, time, and categories as conditions of human experience, while leaving noumena in a restricted role.

## German idealism

In the decades after **Kant**, **Fichte**, **Schelling**, and **G. W. F. Hegel** developed systematic philosophies in which self-consciousness, freedom, or Geist structured reality and history. **Hegel** claimed that the real is rational, a formula later readers interpreted in divergent political and metaphysical ways.

## Idealism today

Contemporary philosophers rarely defend **Berkeleyan** immaterialism wholesale, but debates about structural realism, idealism about morality or mathematics, and **Kantian** themes keep idealist arguments alive in specialized form.
