## Overview

Beauty has been a central topic in philosophy and art since [[ancient-greece]]. [[Plato]] treated it as a Form—something that transcends particular beautiful things and can be grasped by reason. Later thinkers linked beauty to proportion, harmony, [[mathematical-proportion]], or to subjective response. The concept bridges [[art]], ethics, and metaphysics: how we judge what is beautiful and what role beauty plays in a good life.

## Key Ideas

- **Platonic beauty**: In the *Symposium* and *Phaedrus*, [[Plato]] presents beauty as a Form that draws the soul toward truth and the good. Love of beautiful things can be a step toward love of beauty itself.
- **Form and proportion**: Classical and [[Renaissance]] thought often tied beauty to order, symmetry, and mathematical ratio—ideas that influenced art and architecture for centuries.
- **Beauty and truth**: The idea that beauty and truth are related (e.g. “Beauty is truth, truth beauty”) recurs in philosophy and poetry and echoes Platonic themes.
- **Subjectivity and taste**: Modern aesthetics has stressed the role of perception, culture, and individual taste, while still debating whether some judgments of beauty can be more apt than others.

## Connections

- **[[Plato]]** – Form of beauty; beauty as path to the good; critique of [[imitation]] in art.
- **[[Renaissance]]** – Revival of classical ideals of proportion and beauty in art theory.
- **[[mathematical-proportion]]** – Often invoked in theories of beauty in nature and art.
