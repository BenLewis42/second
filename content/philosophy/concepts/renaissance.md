## Overview

The Renaissance was a period of renewed interest in the literature, art, and philosophy of [[ancient-greece]] and Rome. It began in Italy and spread across Europe, shifting emphasis from medieval scholasticism toward human-centered inquiry, [[observation]], and the natural world. In art and philosophy, the Renaissance revived [[Plato]] and [[Aristotle]], stressed [[mathematical-proportion]] and ideal form, and encouraged the union of art with science—exemplified by figures like [[Leonardo da Vinci]].

## Key Ideas

- **Humanism**: Focus on human potential, dignity, and reason rather than solely religious authority. Education in the classics (literature, history, moral philosophy) was seen as the path to a full life.
- **Revival of antiquity**: Rediscovery and translation of Greek and Roman texts. [[Plato]]’s ideas, especially on [[beauty]] and the Forms, influenced art theory and the idea of capturing ideal nature.
- **Art and science together**: Painting, sculpture, and architecture were studied with geometry, anatomy, and optics. Artists sought to represent the world accurately and beautifully, in line with both observation and classical ideals.
- **Renaissance humanism**: A broad educational and cultural program—rhetoric, ethics, history—that shaped court and civic life and supported the arts.

## Connections

- **[[Plato]]** – Platonic Forms and [[beauty]] influenced Renaissance aesthetics and the pursuit of ideal proportion.
- **[[Leonardo da Vinci]]** – Embodied the Renaissance ideal of combining art, science, and observation.
- **[[mathematical-proportion]]** – Central to Renaissance art theory and practice.
