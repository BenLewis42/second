## Overview

Amongst its central questions are the difference between science and non-science, the reliability of scientific theories, and the ultimate purpose and meaning of science as a human endeavor. It overlaps with metaphysics, ontology, logic, and [[epistemology]], for example, when it explores the relationship between science and the concept of truth. Many of the central problems lack contemporary consensus, including whether science can infer truth about unobservable entities and whether inductive reasoning can be justified as yielding definite scientific knowledge.

## Historical Development

While philosophical thought pertaining to science dates back at least to Aristotle, the general philosophy of science emerged as a distinct discipline only in the 20th century following the **logical positivist** movement, which aimed to formulate criteria for ensuring all philosophical statements' meaningfulness and objectively assessing them.

- **Karl Popper** criticized logical positivism and helped establish a modern set of standards for scientific methodology.
- **Thomas Kuhn's** 1962 *The Structure of Scientific Revolutions* challenged the view of scientific progress as the steady, cumulative acquisition of knowledge, arguing instead that progress is relative to a "paradigm" — the set of questions, concepts, and practices that define a scientific discipline in a particular historical period.
- **W. V. Quine** and the coherentist approach: a theory is validated if it makes sense of observations as part of a coherent whole.
- **Stephen Jay Gould** sought to ground science in axiomatic assumptions, such as the uniformity of nature.
- **Paul Feyerabend** argued against the existence of "the scientific method," claiming all approaches to science should be allowed, including explicitly supernatural ones.
- The **sociology of knowledge** approach, represented by David Bloor and Barry Barnes, studies how knowledge is created from a sociological perspective.

A central theme is whether the terms of one scientific theory can be intra- or intertheoretically reduced to the terms of another. Can chemistry be reduced to physics, or can sociology be reduced to individual psychology?

## Deductive Reasoning

The process of drawing valid inferences. An inference is valid if its conclusion follows logically from its premises — it is impossible for the premises to be true and the conclusion to be false. For example: from "all men are mortal" and "[[Socrates]] is a man" to "Socrates is mortal."

- **Semantic approach**: an argument is valid if there is no possible interpretation whereby its premises are true and its conclusion is false.
- **Syntactic approach**: focuses on rules of inference (modus ponens, modus tollens, etc.)
- **Formal fallacies**: invalid deductive arguments that do not follow a rule of inference.

## Inductive Reasoning

Methods of reasoning in which broad generalizations or principles are derived from a body of observations. Unlike deductive reasoning where the conclusion is certain given the premises, the truth of an inductive conclusion is at best probable, based upon the evidence given.
