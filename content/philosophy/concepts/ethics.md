## Overview
Ethics asks how we ought to live and act, and whether moral claims are objective, subjective, or socially constructed. From the virtue ethics of [[ancient-greece]] to Kantian deontology to [[friedrich-nietzsche]]'s genealogical assault on moral foundations, the discipline has repeatedly dissolved and reconstructed its own ground. In the [[19th-century]], ethics became entangled with questions of [[power]] and history in ways that permanently complicated the Enlightenment project of grounding morality in universal reason. This matters for art and philosophy because aesthetic judgment has always shadowed moral judgment — [[beauty]] and goodness were frequently treated as cognate concepts.

## Key Ideas
[[socrates]] established that ethics is a matter of rigorous inquiry rather than inherited convention, and that living well requires self-knowledge. [[friedrich-nietzsche]]'s critique went further, arguing that conventional morality is itself an expression of [[power]] — specifically the *ressentiment* of the weak — and must be revalued from the ground up. This move made ethics genealogical rather than foundational: instead of asking what is right, Nietzsche asked whose values these are and what drives them. The relationship between [[metaphysics]] and ethics is contested: some traditions (Platonic, Kantian) ground moral obligation in a metaphysical account of reason or Form, while [[existentialism]] insists that values must be created in the absence of such foundations.
