## Overview

Aesthetics is the branch of philosophy that studies [[beauty]], art, and taste—how we perceive, judge, and value sensory experience and artistic creation. It connects [[art]] practice with philosophical questions about representation, expression, and the good. From [[Plato]] and [[Aristotle]] through the [[Renaissance]] and into modern theory, aesthetics has shaped how we think about what art is and why it matters.

## Key Ideas

- **Classical roots**: [[Plato]] was wary of art as imitation of the sensible world (itself an imitation of the [[forms]]), but allowed that beauty could lead the soul toward truth. [[Aristotle]]’s *Poetics* addressed tragedy and catharsis, influencing later theories of art and emotion.
- **Taste and judgment**: In the 18th century, aesthetics emerged as a distinct field (Baumgarten, Kant). Questions of taste—whether it is subjective or has standards—remain central. [[Beauty]] is often treated as either a property of objects or a response of the perceiver.
- **Art and philosophy**: Aesthetics links to [[ethics]] (can art be morally good or bad?), [[metaphysics]] (what kind of thing is a work of art?), and to the history of [[art]] and [[Renaissance]] ideals of proportion and harmony.
