## Overview
Power enters Western philosophy at two distinct registers. As a metaphysical concept, it names the capacity for activity and self-assertion — Aristotle's dunamis, Spinoza's conatus, and most consequentially [[friedrich-nietzsche]]'s **will to power**, which recasts all living processes as expressions of striving for growth and self-overcoming. As a political and ethical concept, power names the asymmetric relationships between persons and institutions that shape what is possible, permitted, and thinkable. [[friedrich-nietzsche]] is the pivot figure who connects these registers: his claim that morality, knowledge, and [[metaphysics]] are all conditioned by relations of [[power]] made power the central explanatory concept for much of subsequent continental philosophy.

## Key Ideas
Nietzsche's will to power is not primarily about domination over others but about self-mastery and overcoming internal resistance — though its political misreadings are historically significant and philosophically instructive about how concepts acquire lives independent of their authors' intentions. [[german-philosophy]] in the [[19th-century]] theorized power in multiple registers simultaneously: Hegel's dialectic of master and slave gave power a structural role in the constitution of self-consciousness; Marx analyzed economic [[power]] as the material basis of ideology. [[existentialism]] inherited the Nietzschean problem: if [[ethics]] cannot appeal to [[metaphysics|metaphysical]] foundations that transcend human power-relations, on what grounds can we critique domination rather than simply affirming the stronger? This remains an open question.
