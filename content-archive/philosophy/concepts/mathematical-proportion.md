## Overview
The Renaissance conviction that [[beauty]] is an objective property expressible in numerical ratios drew directly on Pythagorean and Platonic traditions, filtered through Vitruvius and classical statuary. Mathematical proportion allowed art theory to claim scientific legitimacy: if ideal form can be expressed as ratio, then [[art]] becomes a form of knowledge rather than mere craft or decoration. [[leonardo-da-vinci]] exemplifies the fusion most completely, integrating proportional systems with [[anatomy|anatomical]] observation to produce figures that claim both mathematical regularity and empirical accuracy.

## Key Ideas
The Vitruvian system described the human body as a source of architectural proportion — the circle and square that circumscribe the ideal human figure encode cosmic order in organic form. [[plato]]'s account of [[beauty]] as harmony and proportion gave this a metaphysical foundation: proportion in sensory form participates in the intelligible order of the Forms. [[leonardo-da-vinci]]'s anatomical drawings are inseparable from proportional analysis — he measured and compared body segments to establish the rational scaffold beneath the visible surface. The tension between ideal proportion and observed variation is one of the productive motors of [[renaissance]] art, generating both canonical figural norms and the interest in expressive deviation from them.
