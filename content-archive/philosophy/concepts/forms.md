## Overview

The Theory of Forms (or Ideas) is a central doctrine in [[Plato]]'s philosophy. Forms are perfect, unchanging, non-physical entities that exist in a realm beyond the sensible world. What we perceive are only imperfect copies or participants in these Forms. The concept shaped Western metaphysics and aesthetics for centuries and influenced how [[Renaissance]] artists thought about ideal proportion and beauty.

## Key Ideas

Plato held that the material world is constantly changing and knowable only through the senses, while the realm of Forms is eternal and knowable through reason. The Form of the Good stands at the top of the hierarchy. Mathematical objects and moral qualities (e.g. justice, beauty) have Forms; physical things imitate them. In the [[beauty]] entry, beauty in the world is explained by participation in the Form of Beauty. This view links [[metaphysics]] and [[epistemology]]: true knowledge is of Forms, not of shifting particulars.
