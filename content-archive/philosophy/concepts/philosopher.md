## Overview
Philosophy has always theorized its own practitioners, and the figure of the [[philosopher]] has been remarkably contested — serene contemplator of Forms, gadfly provocateur, systematic scientist of concepts, or anti-systematic critic of all systematizing. [[plato]] offered the most influential early model: the philosopher is the one who escapes the cave of appearances and achieves genuine knowledge of the Forms, and whose knowledge therefore qualifies him to rule. [[socrates]] complicated this immediately by exemplifying a philosophy that produces not doctrine but perpetual questioning. [[friedrich-nietzsche]]'s philosopher of the future is defined not by systematic knowledge but by the courage to create new values in the absence of inherited foundations.

## Key Ideas
[[plato]]'s philosopher-king remains the most politically consequential conception: the claim that genuine knowledge of [[ethics]] and [[metaphysics]] grounds legitimate authority runs through Platonic political philosophy and its critics. [[socrates]]' actual practice diverged from this — his philosophy undermined rather than installed authority, which is why Athens executed him. In [[german-philosophy]], the philosopher's social role became explicitly theorized: Hegel's philosopher comprehends the rational structure of history; Marx's philosopher of praxis changes it rather than merely interpreting it. [[friedrich-nietzsche]]'s revaluation made the philosopher a legislator of values whose authority is a form of [[power]] rather than knowledge, anticipating [[existentialism]]'s insistence on individual self-creation.
