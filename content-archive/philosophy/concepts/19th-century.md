## Overview
The 19th century transformed Western philosophy by moving away from the confident systematizing of the [[Enlightenment]] toward a reckoning with history, power, and the limits of reason. Figures such as [[friedrich-nietzsche]], Hegel, Marx, and Schopenhauer redefined the questions [[philosophy]] could ask, shifting the center of gravity from epistemology toward [[ethics]], [[metaphysics]] of will, and social critique. This period matters in art and philosophy alike because it produced the vocabulary — dialectic, will to power, alienation, the Übermensch — that 20th-century thought could not escape. The tension between idealist systems and anti-systematic revolt defines the century's intellectual character.

## Key Ideas
The era's defining move was historicism: the recognition that ideas, morality, and institutions are products of time rather than eternal truths. [[german-philosophy]] in particular developed this through Hegel's dialectic, the thesis that [[metaphysics]] and history are inseparable. Against this, [[existentialism]] emerged as a protest — [[friedrich-nietzsche]] attacked systematic philosophy as a cover for [[power]] and ressentiment. The century also saw [[ethics]] become explicitly political, with utilitarian and Marxist frameworks insisting that moral philosophy must account for material conditions. In aesthetics, [[art]] was increasingly theorized as the site where rational and irrational forces collide, anticipating 20th-century modernism.
