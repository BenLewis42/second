## Overview
Existentialism is less a unified doctrine than a cluster of related revolts against philosophical systematizing and the assumption that essence precedes existence — that human beings have a fixed nature or telos that determines how they ought to live. Its roots lie in the [[19th-century]] responses to idealism, particularly [[friedrich-nietzsche]]'s proclamation that God is dead and that values must be created rather than discovered. The movement matters in philosophy because it reorients the central questions: instead of asking what reality is like in general ([[metaphysics]]) or what universal moral laws hold ([[ethics]]), it asks what it means for a particular individual to exist, choose, and take responsibility.

## Key Ideas
[[friedrich-nietzsche]]'s contribution is foundational even where later existentialists diverged from him: his insistence on the death of God, the will to [[power]], and the necessity of self-overcoming established the terms of the problem. The concept of **bad faith** — living inauthentically by pretending one lacks freedom — became central to 20th-century existentialism. Anxiety functions as a philosophical disclosure in this tradition: it reveals freedom rather than threatening it, since anxiety arises precisely when no predetermined script dictates the right action. The relationship between existentialism and [[ethics]] is permanently tense — if values are created rather than discovered, the question of which values to create has no external answer, only the weight of individual commitment.
