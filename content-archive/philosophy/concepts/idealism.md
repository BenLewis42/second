## Overview

Idealism in philosophy is the view that reality is ultimately mental, conceptual, or grounded in ideas rather than in independent matter. [[Plato]]'s [[forms]] are an early version: the true reality is the realm of Forms, not the physical world. Later idealists (e.g. Berkeley, Hegel) developed different versions. The term also appears in art and aesthetics when discussing idealised representation or the priority of idea over mere imitation.

## Key Ideas

Platonic idealism holds that the world we see is a shadow of intelligible [[forms]]. Knowledge of these Forms is achieved through reason and dialectic, not sense experience. In [[epistemology]], idealism can mean that what we know is structured by mind or ideas. In art, "idealism" often means depicting subjects in an improved or normative way, influenced by classical and [[Renaissance]] thought about ideal proportion and [[beauty]].

## Connections

Referenced by: [[plato]], [[ancient-greece]], [[epistemology]]. Idealism contrasts with materialism and with [[Aristotle]]'s immanent forms. It remains important in [[metaphysics]] and philosophy of mind.
